## **The Experiment Setup**

We created two versions of the same ad. The first version featured a real human face, while the second used an AI-generated face. Everything else stayed the same: the headline, the offer, the caption, the audience targeting, and the budget split.

The goal was simple: compare performance fairly and see whether people responded differently when the visual looked human versus AI-created. We tracked key metrics like impressions, click-through rate, engagement, and lead quality to understand what the audience actually preferred.

This kind of testing is especially useful for brands investing in [**top digital marketing services**](http://www.growthspare.com/) because it moves the conversation from opinion to evidence. Instead of guessing what works, marketers can test, measure, and optimize based on real user behavior.

## **What We Observed**

The human-face ads felt more familiar and approachable. People seemed to trust it faster, and the ad created a stronger emotional connection, especially when the messaging was tied to a relatable problem or outcome.

The AI-face ad, however, attracted attention for a different reason. It looked different enough to make people pause, and in some cases that curiosity drove clicks. But attention alone did not always translate into deeper engagement or stronger leads.

The biggest lesson was that AI visuals may generate interest, but human visuals often create comfort. For service-based businesses, especially those searching for **SEO services** or a reliable marketing partner, comfort can matter more than novelty.

## **Where AI Visuals Help**

For campaigns where the focus is novelty, fast production, or concept testing, AI can be an efficient option. It can also help marketers explore new directions before committing to full-scale creative development.

But for service businesses, there is a catch. If the ad is supposed to build trust, AI visuals can sometimes feel too polished, too generic, or too detached from reality.
